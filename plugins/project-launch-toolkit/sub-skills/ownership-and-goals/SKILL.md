# Ownership and Goals Sub-Skill

Build the ownership and responsibilities table: assign who owns what, decompose the project-level value hypothesis into per-person client value-creation goals, and capture each person's professional-development goals. Ownership is a core Newry principle — the launch is where every workstream gets a clear owner.

## Inputs

**Required:**
- Value creation hypothesis (from the value-creation sub-skill, or provided directly)
- Activity list (from the workplan sub-skill — what work each issue needs; the basis for assigning responsibilities)
- Team roster with roles (ED, EM, consultant names)

**Optional:**
- Team member learning objectives (from the team goals discussion — if already held, incorporate them)
- Individual consultant experience level (informs realistic goal-setting)

## The KPI Scorecard (Value to Newry)

Goals are scored at project close against Newry's four-category scorecard. Connect each person's goals to one or more:

1. **People Development** — What skills or knowledge will this person gain that can be independently leveraged on future projects?
2. **Firm Capabilities Development** — What new capability, resource, or tool will be created that the firm can reuse?
3. **Value Delivery** — What specifically will this person contribute to the client deliverable?
4. **Process Adherence** — What project execution standards will this person own or model?

Client-relationship ownership is real and important, but it isn't a separate scored category — capture it under **Responsibilities** here, and use the **account-management** sub-skill for the relationship plan itself.

**Read the method first.** Before drafting goals, read `references/project initiation resources/2025 CLST Workplanning (Module 1 and 2).md` (KPI scoring criteria + delegation principles) so goals map to how they'll be scored at project close.

## What This Skill Does

**Step 1 — Open the conversation**

Ask: "Would you like help drafting the ownership and value creation goals for this project?"

If yes, ask two focused questions (one at a time):
1. Is any workstream ownership already decided?
2. Are there development objectives you already know about for specific team members?

Then proceed.

**Step 2 — Present the responsibility matrix**

Ask: "Here's a suggested workstream breakdown based on the workplan — would you like to assign ownership this way?"

Present an editable responsibility matrix artifact:
- **Rows** — suggested workstreams from the workplan (pre-populated; EM can rename, add, delete, or split rows freely)
- **Columns** — one per team member, populated from the roster
- **Cells** — default display is Lead / Support / — (dropdown); clicking a cell opens a popover with the dropdown at top and a free-text field below for detailed responsibility description
- **"Add workstream" button** at the bottom for rows not in the workplan suggestion

Wait for the EM to confirm or edit the matrix before proceeding.

**Step 3 — Draft value creation goals**

For each team member, synthesize:
- The overall client value hypothesis (what the project is creating for the client)
- That person's specific responsibilities from the confirmed matrix

Draft a concrete value creation goal that shows how this person's work moves the needle on the client value story. The goal should be specific — not "conduct interviews" but "validate or refute the core market capture assumption through primary research, the key variable in the value hypothesis." Quantify if it makes sense; directional is fine if quantification would be forced.

Present the draft per person and invite the EM to react. Iterate until the EM owns it.

**Step 4 — Draft professional development goals**

Ask the EM for any known development objectives (if not already provided in Step 1). Supplement with role-appropriate suggestions calibrated to the person's responsibilities:
- Consultants: specific skill or experience they'll build on this project
- EMs: team development, client presence, process discipline
- EDs: relationship deepening, firm capability creation

Goals should be specific. "Improve interviewing skills" is not a goal. "Conduct 8 primary interviews independently by Week 4" is.

**Step 5 — Produce the ownership table**

Compile the confirmed matrix + goals into the final ownership table and write to Airtable if the project record exists.

```
OWNERSHIP & GOALS — [Client Name] — [Project Code]

Overall value hypothesis: [one-line summary]

| Role | Name | Responsibilities | Client Value Creation Goals | Professional Development Goals |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ED | [Name] | [from matrix] | [drafted goal] | [drafted goal] |
| EM | [Name] | [from matrix] | [drafted goal] | [drafted goal] |
| Team | [Name] | [from matrix] | [drafted goal] | [drafted goal] |

NOTES:
- [Any assumptions made, aspirational goals flagged, or EM direction captured]
```

## Design Notes

- **Value creation goals are a synthesis, not a description.** They connect the person's specific work to the client value story — not a restatement of their responsibilities. This is the connection teams consistently miss.
- **Goals should be specific, not generic.** "Improve interviewing skills" is not a goal. "Conduct 8 primary interviews independently (without EM on the call) by Week 4" is.
- **Role level determines goal type, not just scope.** ED goals are mostly relational and strategic; EM goals span execution, team development, and process; consultant goals are specific workstream contributions plus personal development.
- **Learning objectives inform delegation.** If a consultant wants experience in financial modeling, their workstream assignment should include a modeling component even if another team member is more capable. This is by design.
- **These goals are co-owned, not handed down.** Instruct the EM to review with each team member individually before kickoff. The skill produces a draft; the conversation makes them real.
- **Goals will be used in EPEs/EMEs.** Specificity matters — vague goals can't be scored at project close.
