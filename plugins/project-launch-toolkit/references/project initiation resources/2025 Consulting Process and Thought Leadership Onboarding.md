## The Newry Project Cycle

1. **Project Inception** — Build client relationship, set general objectives, set fees
2. **Scope and Plan** — Detailed issue analysis; create work plan, roles, team assignments; develop expense budget; share detailed plan at kickoff
3. **Primary & Secondary Research** — Conduct research against objectives; develop interim presentations and preliminary findings; manage and lead team
4. **Synthesize & Communicate** — Synthesize findings from all workstreams; develop final communications
5. **Develop Findings and Recs.** — Augment research to refine insights and conclusions
6. **Follow Up** — Check in with client; provide performance feedback; participate in AER if applicable

## Problem Solving Process

DEFINE PROBLEM → STRUCTURE PROBLEM → PRIORITIZE ISSUES → PLAN WORK → CONDUCT ANALYSES → SYNTHESIZE FINDINGS → DEVELOP RECS.

The 80/20 rule applies throughout.

## Defining the Problem

**What is a problem statement?**
Brief description that clearly defines a problem to be solved and what success will look like. Reinforced by stakeholders, risks, background.

**Why write a problem statement?**
- Acts as a "north star" to guide the overall engagement
- Limits scope creep by aligning team and client on expected outcomes
- Accelerates workflow by identifying resource needs and surfacing obstacles
- Describes the ideal state the client is seeking

**SMART objective criteria:**
- Specific: What and where is the primary focus?
- Measurable: How much are we delivering?
- Actionable/Achievable: Can we take action to get there?
- Relevant: How is this related to the client's overall priorities?
- Time-bound: When will the team complete the work?

**Problem Statement template fields:**
- Client Business Context (situation and complication)
- Stakeholders (Sponsor, Direct Contact, Others)
- Scope (In scope / Out of scope / Preferred accuracy level)
- Project Objective (SMART)
- Risks
- Key Client Milestones

**Tips:**
- PS helps team identify key questions for client at kickoff
- Review PS with any team members added to the team
- Use PS to get editorial team or peer reviewers up to speed quickly
- EM/Lead Consultant should draft PS prior to Project Initiation meeting and share with team

## Structuring the Problem (Issue Tree)

**What is an issue tree?**
A structured way of breaking the problem into a logical series of component parts.

**Why use an issue tree?**
- Ensures integrity of the problem-solving effort
- Builds common understanding of the framework
- Helps focus team efforts

**MECE tips:**
- Tackle one level at a time — complete and verify MECE before going deeper
- Develop a first draft quickly — iterate rapidly

**RWW framework (Real-Win-Worth)** — commonly adapted for innovation/growth engine projects.

## Prioritizing Issues

**How to prioritize:**
- Impact, ease of impact, urgency, fit with mission, strategic alignment, fit with capabilities, option value
- Identify "knockout analyses" — test for potential deathblows first

## Plan Analyses & Work (Workplanning)

**Workplanning tips:**
- For everything you do, anticipate: "The answer to this activity will look like X and tell us Y"
- No interviewing in a vacuum just to beef up the count
- Always ask yourself: Why am I doing this? Who cares? So what?

**Issue → Analysis → Hypothesis → Conclusions → Recommendations → Workplan Activities chain** ties the whole project together.

**Elements of a successful workplan:**
- Staffing/roles/assignments (including development goals)
- Time management (maps activities to timeline; allows for reviews and feedback)
- Expense budget (interviews, travel, reports, SMEs)

**Workplanning considerations (the 5 S's):**
- Severity: Prioritize answering the most urgent and critical questions
- Sequencing: Order activities logically — do "knock-out" analyses first
- Schedules: Take vacations, holidays, and other availability into account
- Skillsets: Assignments should consider development goals and interests
- Sanity: Always ask whether the timeline is actually feasible

**Bonus:** The final update is not your deadline. Always plan ahead for EM/ED reviews, editorial review, rehearsal, facilitation planning.

## Project Initiation Guidance

- Teams should allow ~1 hour for a team discussion on each of: Problem Statement, Issue Tree, and Workplan, plus additional time for setting individual, firm, and client goals
- Shorter projects may not require extensive project initiation; EDs provide guidance
- An Editor should be invited to every Project Initiation meeting
- EM/Lead Consultant should review proposals and scoping materials and draft PS, Issue Tree, and Workplan prior to meeting, then share with team
- Think creatively about how to leverage AI tools as an additional "team member"

## Standard Weekly Team Meeting

**Agenda:**
1. Scorecard — quickly review (filled out by EM before meeting)
2. SoF — review and revise current version (EM and team members draft and compare)
3. Issues — share current/anticipated issues; prioritize and problem-solve
4. Workplan — review and set next steps and specific activities for the week

Weekly summary also provided in Slack by EM or Lead Consultant.

## One-Day Answer & Dot-Dash Memo

**One-Day Answer:** Current best analysis at any given moment. Classic structure: Situation → Complication/Observation → Resolution. Later in project: lead with "you should do X" and follow with supporting points.

**Dot-Dash Memo:** Longer-form one-day answer; quick way to iterate on issue tree; useful within team and with client.

---

## Synthesis

**Facts vs. Insight:**
- Facts: raw observations
- Interesting Summary: organized facts
- Insightful Synthesis: "Torrey enjoys the game of baseball" (inference, not just description)

Always ask "Why?" and "So what?" Keep asking yourself: What is most important from the client's perspective?

---

## Communications (Pyramid Principle)

- Overall Conclusion → Main Messages → Supporting Points
- Each idea at any level summarizes the ideas grouped below it
- Groups must be MECE
- The final communications should always link back to the underlying logic and issue tree
- A flawless issue tree → flawless communication
