## Focus: Steps 2–3 of the 7-Step Problem Solving Process

1. Define the problem
2. **Structure the problem** ← focus
3. **Prioritize issues** ← focus
4. Plan analyses and work
5. Conduct analyses
6. Synthesize findings
7. Develop recommendations

---

## Problem Statement Template

- **Background:** What is the client's overall business goal on this topic? Has the client had previous attempts or successes?
- **Stakeholders:** Sponsor, sponsor's goals, direct contact, direct contact's goals, other stakeholders/goals
- **Success:** What information is needed for client action on recommendations? How can we go above and beyond?
- **Client Assumptions:** What does the client believe to be true? Why? How are we testing those beliefs?
- **Scope:** In scope / Out of scope / Client's preferred level of accuracy
- **Team Objective**

## What Is an Issue Tree and Why Is It Important?

A problem solving tool that breaks a problem into discrete chunks.

**Why use issue trees?**
- To break a problem into small enough chunks to enable analysis, prioritization, and team focus
- To build a common understanding within the team of the problem solving framework
- To open up the solution space to see new angles

**Issue trees can be used to answer:**
- Why questions (why have sales decreased?)
- How questions (how can we grow revenues?)
- What questions (what markets should we target?)

---

## MECE — The Core Issue Tree Best Practice

**Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive**
- No overlap — more than one cannot be true
- No gaps — at least one must be true

---

## Example Issue Tree: How can we increase sales?

```
Increase sales
├── Increase sales per customer
│   ├── Change customer mix
│   ├── Increase part of customers' supplies
│   └── Increase clients' potential
└── Increase number of customers
    ├── Gain new customer groups
    ├── Gain individual customer groups
    └── Decrease fluctuation of customers
```

## Prioritization

- Take a quick, informal approach to get started — go after the "quick wins"
- Use likely impact to decide where to go first
- Simplify — sometimes a "back of the envelope" calculation is all that's needed
- Iterate — if new information emerges, reprioritize
- Use readily available data; avoid major data requests
