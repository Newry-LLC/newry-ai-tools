## Key Framework: Issue Tree → Hypothesis → Analyses → Insights → Conclusions → Recommendations → Workplan Activities

During projects, iterate continuously in this format:

| Column | Content |
|---|---|
| Key Issues | Yes/no questions at each branch |
| Hypotheses | Stated likely resolution with rationale |
| Analyses | Work needed to prove/disprove |
| Insights | What the analysis found |
| Conclusions | So what? |
| Recommendations | What the client should do |
| Workplan Activities | Specific tasks tied to each issue |

Focus on issues/hypotheses/analyses early; shift to conclusions/recs later.

## RWW Framework (Real-Win-Worth)

Often adapted for innovation/growth engine projects:

```
Should we pursue opportunity X?
├── X is real
│   ├── The market is real (need exists, customer can/will buy, market big enough)
│   └── The technology is real (concept can be made, will satisfy requirements)
├── We can win
│   ├── The product can be competitive (handle response, sustainable advantage)
│   └── The company can be competitive (understand market, superior resources)
└── It is worth it
    ├── The product will be profitable at acceptable risk (returns > costs)
    └── Launching makes strategic sense (top mgmt support, fits strategy)
```

## Real Project Example — Stellex (Crest) Issue Tree

```
What are Crest's best opportunities for growth?
├── Where is Crest already selling/growing today?
│   ├── Current sales/growth pipeline
│   └── Landscape by business, industry, application, customer
├── Where can additional aftermarket sales be made?
├── Where are there cross-selling opportunities between businesses?
├── Which potential opportunities are largest?
└── What adjacent markets are attractive?
    ├── Which adjacencies align with current capabilities?
    ├── Which contain existing customer relationships?
    └── Where does Crest have best competitive edge?
```

## Issue Tree Development Tips

- Tackle one level at a time — complete and verify MECE before going deeper
- Develop a first draft quickly — iterate rapidly, don't agonize
- Think RIGOR and INSIGHT — does the structure create early insight into the answer?

## Issue Prioritization Tips

- Keep it simple — use impact and ease of impact as primary criteria
- Recognize impact is not always quantifiable (e.g., client competitiveness)
- Think IMPACT and EFFICIENCY — what questions are most critical? What can we skip?

## Work Planning Tips (from this session)

- Tie issues, analyses, and end products — one-to-one linkage between issues/hypotheses and end products/analyses
- Ensure balanced workload
- Consider three workplan horizons: (1) end of engagement, (2) key progress reviews, (3) daily/weekly team meetings

## Conducting Analyses Tips

- Focus on answers, not analysis — never "just run the numbers"
- Simpler is sometimes better — 20% of analysis yields 80% of solution

## Nuanced Considerations by Project Type

- **Growth Engine vs. Innovation:** Establish clear metrics to judge growth areas early; Growth Engine often focuses on multiple businesses/customers
- **New vs. existing clients:** Spend more time sharing project framing (issue tree, PS) for new clients; can "dive in" more quickly with existing clients
- **Fuzzy vs. narrow scope:** When scope is amorphous, define clear success criteria early and remain flexible
- **Large vs. small team:** Large teams need more time to launch; small teams can use AI as a thought partner
- **Early vs. late stage product/technology:** Early stage = exploration and challenging assumptions; late stage = refining value prop and go-to-market
