## PRE-INTERVIEW

### Who to Interview
- Be systematic to maximize ROI; develop an interview strategy at the start
- Map value chain and market segments; identify top players in each category
- Categories of targets: SMEs (academia, research labs, trade associations, industry consultants), suppliers, customers, distributors, competitors
- Define a plan using the output and answer you're driving toward, then define who you need to talk to and the topic
- Map out a profile of experts (titles, companies, industry); include mix from different customer segments, geographies, job titles
- Identify potential companies via conference attendee lists; review industry association websites
- Verify market coverage by comparing interview list to industry market shares
- Consider academics/government agencies for macro perspective

### Interview Guide (developed during sourcing)
- Reflect objectives from the issue tree; tailor by audience
- Include: Career Path & Expertise, Technology & Trends, Industry Landscape, Technical Deep-Dives, Advice & Resources, Referral/Networking, Logistics & Etiquette
- Describe purpose, expected duration; ask if they have questions before the interview begins

**AlphaSights/Guidepoint attribution rule:** Cannot list the name of the expert; refer to as "Anonymous" but include title and company.

### How Many to Contact
- Aim for more interviews than you need — some will be unavailable or refuse
- Be mindful of time of year: low response around holidays; add 2–3 weeks for European summer
- Consider profile: CEOs are harder to land than academics or managers
- Highly specialized or sensitive subjects (military & defense) will yield lower response rates
- Keep track of each outreach in your Excel or Airtable contact list

### Contact List Best Practices
- Track in Airtable or Excel using standard column headers
- If multiple team members contacting experts, check for duplicates
- Update status throughout; characterize segments to track breadth
- Include links/notes to sources as reminder of why interested
- Add keywords column to describe the expert
- Indicate Super SMEs: high-quality experts used repeatedly or recently

### Outreach Sequence
**Step 1:** Intro email (always email first; InMail second)
**Step 2:** Follow-up email or phone call (based on industry); count on leaving a voicemail
**Step 3:** Close email — reference previous attempts; still at the bottom of the mountain, keep following up

Additional options if critical: reach out to admin; ask for alternative resource if too busy

### Accelerating the Process
- Reach out to expert networks on Day 1; send detailed description of what you're asking for
- Cold-calling is tough but sometimes works, particularly in non-sensitive areas; salespeople will almost always give a sales pitch
- Grounding/general industry interviews can be done sooner; academics and journal article authors are easy
- Batch workflow: ID contacts in AM, blast reach outs over lunch; start blasting Week 1 if not Day 1
- Microsoft will flag your email account if you send too many emails — consider typing instead of copying/pasting

### Introductory Email Components
- **Subject line:** Specific purpose (e.g., "Request for discussion of Thermoelectric Generators for Autos")
- **Introduction:** Identify yourself and why you're interested; compelling statement of why they should speak with you; use client name early if permitted
- **Details:** Specific objectives and topics; no more than one paragraph/paragraph plus bullets
- **Close:** Where/when; build in flexibility; express appreciation; mention incentive discreetly

**Tips:**
- Don't be generic — tailor specifically to project and contact; express specific interest in their work; emphasize them not you; drop names/referrals
- Shorter is better
- Appeal to them as an essential perspective for the project
- Scrutinize language and tone via peer review
- Sincerely express interest in adding value for them — a two-way conversation
- Consider formal vs. casual tone based on recipient

### Email Examples

**Unblinded:**
> Hi Matthew, I'm working with DuPont to develop new applications for high-temperature polymer films, and we're looking at insulation films for laminated busbars. I understand you design busbars, and I was hoping to get your perspectives. We're happy to compensate you for your time. Would you have time for a brief call to discuss? I'm trying to understand how insulation films are specified, how different films perform today, and if there's need or interest in new films. I know some DuPont materials are already used, so it would be great to understand if/where there are performance gaps. Any guidance you can provide would be very helpful. Let me know if there's a good time for a brief call. Again, happy to compensate you for your time.

**Blinded:**
> James, My name is Lucy, and I work with Newry, a strategy consulting firm specializing in guiding technology-driven clients towards their growth objectives. I came across your LinkedIn profile and noticed your expertise in solar engineering, particularly in satellite arrays and batteries. Your background seems like an excellent fit for a consulting project I'm working on. I'm interested in learning more about satellite PV systems, specifically those with solar panels featuring cover glass designed for space operations. Please note that I am not seeking any confidential information. Specifically, I'd like to understand more about the following factors: the design and manufacturing process of a satellite including the array and solar covers, the decision-making process for how components are selected and sourced, and more about the life of a satellite and performance issues. Would you be available for an information gathering call this week to discuss these topics? We would be glad to compensate you for your time, if suitable.

**Sales-oriented:**
> Dear Joe, I hope you're doing well! My name is Jane Doe, and I'm a Growth Consultant with Newry, a consulting firm specializing in the chemical industry. I came across your profile on LinkedIn and wanted to reach out regarding a potential collaboration. My client is a U.S.-based distributor of acrylic monomers and tackifiers, with a growing focus on supporting industries impacted by PFAS regulations. Given their current offering of DADMAC, water treatment applications stood out as a natural fit. I understand that SNF is currently the largest U.S. producer of polydadmac, so I wanted to check if you might be experiencing any supply challenges or constraints. Would you be open to a conversation to explore how my client could assist in meeting your needs? Even if your current supply is sufficient, they would be glad to serve as a secondary source to support your operations.

### Phone Follow-Up Script

| Component | Details | Tips |
|-----------|---------|------|
| Identify yourself/Newry | Re-introduce yourself; ask if good time to talk; reference how you heard of them | Be professional but friendly; chances are they've read your email |
| Identify your ask | Phone or in-person? Time constraints? How much of their time? Anything to offer? | Don't promise benefits you can't deliver |
| Explain project objectives | Emphasize relevance of interviewee | Flattery is good; fawning is bad |
| Ask for help finding ideal interview | Qualify interviewee — "Are you the right person?" | Don't assume the person you identified is best option |
| Request the interview | "Would you be willing to meet for about 45 minutes?" | Be prepared — they might want to conduct the interview on the spot |

## PREPARING

### Pre-Interview Research
- **Interviewee:** How long in current role? Past roles important? Industry thought-leader? Sources: LinkedIn, conferences, AI, university/journal sites
- **Company:** Performing well? In midst of upheaval? Sources: company website, AI tool, YouTube, journal articles
- **Technology/Products:** What do they make? How? Sources: same as company
- **Competitors/Industry:** Primary competitors? New entrants? Regulations? Megatrends? Sources: analyst reports, FactSet, AlphaSense, news articles

Tip: Share synopsis of interviewee bio and previous communication context with team in Slack prior to interview

### LinkedIn Profile Hygiene
- Interviewees often look at your LinkedIn profile before the call
- Add Newry description: "Newry is a growth strategy firm that partners with world-leading innovators and investors to increase their enterprise value."
- Updated headshot; listed as working at Newry; describe accomplishments
- Consider Newry background
- Include profile link in outreach emails — signals legitimacy

### Interview Guide Structure
- **Header:** Project, Interview Subject/Title/Company, contact info, LinkedIn profile, date, Newry interviewers
- **Background:** Company — why you wanted insight from this company; Interviewee — same rationale
- **Interview Objectives:** What do you want/hope to learn?
- **Interview Guide:** Specific questions from issue tree; keep "money slide" / highest-priority questions in front of you

Notes must be clear, comprehensible, and available for: team co-interviewer, current Newry team, current client (who has every right to interview notes), future Newry teams.
Save notes under the Primary Research folder in your project SharePoint drive.

## CONDUCTING

### Keys to a Successful Interview
- **Be Prepared:** Know what information you want and how to get it; know common ground; confirm they can speak to your objectives (expert network calls: AlphaSights/Guidepoint give 5 minutes before charging; AlphaSense gives up to 15 minutes); know what you can/cannot reveal
- **Be Open-Minded:** Let interviewees speak off-topic — you may learn something important; topics they're most excited about can serve as additional data points
- **Be Flexible:** Respect interviewee's time; analyze responses/demeanor and tailor approach (adversarial, disinterested, cooperative?)

### Establishing Rapport
- Introduce yourself; thank interviewee; reference subject; ask if time is still good; remind them you won't pursue confidential/sensitive info; let them know they don't have to answer uncomfortable questions; get names of everyone in attendance
- "Warm up" by asking about professional background — allow plenty of time; don't jump right into questions
- Keep questions brief without too many qualifiers
- Video is often easier for rapport; pay attention to maintaining eye contact (expert networks like Guidepoint, AlphaSights, AlphaSense do not allow video)
- Coordinate with Newry team on who will lead
- Modulate tone: Equals (preferred — makes it a discussion) vs. Student-teacher (some interviewees like pontificating — let them lecture, they may tell you more than they mean to)

### Types of Questions
- **Open questions:** Broad, unstructured ("Tell me about…", "What do you think about…"); use earlier when building rapport, when broaching a threatening topic, building general knowledge base; answers less predictable
- **Closed/directive questions:** Very specific inquiry (how, when, where, why); use later when rapport established and interviewee more comfortable; restricts range of responses; potentially threatening

### Probing Follow-Up Questions

| Probe | Explanation | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Clarify | Didn't understand response or interviewee didn't answer | "I don't understand. Can you explain/say more?" / "Can I play back what I heard?" |
| Elaborate | Answer too naïve or not detailed enough | "Tell me more. Any other reasons?" |
| Interpret | Want interviewee to justify answer | "How do you mean? Can you say more about that?" |
| Challenge | Rarely used, especially by less experienced interviewers | "Let me play devil's advocate…" |
| Triangulate | Mention conflicting viewpoints/data you hope to reconcile | "What we have heard from other sources…" |

Tip: For technical subjects, replay back complex information in your own words — interviewee's corrections will both clarify your understanding and can provide more information than they originally gave.

### Active Listening
- Listen attentively without interruption
- Listen for meaning behind words — look for surprises; listen between the lines
- Let interviewee continue on points they feel are important
- Listen during silence — sometimes interviewees fill it with helpful information
- Follow up by asking for clarity; try same question in a different way; summarize and play back
- Take notes along the way to reference previous answers; having colleagues take notes can help on video calls
- Ask the expert if you can record the call; Teams has a transcript feature

### Redirecting
- Strict adherence to interview guide is not always best practice — it's ok to "follow your nose"
- Gently steer back when interviewees drift; make sure to cover most pressing questions
- Good transitions:
  - "Can I play back what I heard?" → followed by new question
  - "I'd like to circle back to your earlier point on…"
  - "I'm hoping we could change gears and talk about…"

### Handling Hostile Interviewees
- Always be agreeable and respectful — charm may be necessary (not disingenuous flattery); remind them why you want to talk; reiterate benefits to them
- Hold firm — ask them to explain objections; politely remind them they agreed to meet; be credible and prepared; reiterate purpose; remind them they don't have to reveal anything confidential
- Roll with the punches — be prepared for limited patience and time; let them vent; prioritize questions or focus on topics of greatest interest to interviewee

**Rule:** Newry does not interview direct competitors of clients. If conflict becomes evident, gracefully end the interview early and thank the interviewee.

### Closing
- Be aware of when to end: time is up (unless they're engaged and want to continue), you've gotten what you need, they start to lose interest
- Review points covered with interviewee; probe further if time allows
- Ask if they'd recommend anyone else, conferences to attend, key industry publications
- Say thank you; verify availability for follow-up
- Ask if willing to speak again
- Discuss and agree on next steps; if appropriate, remind them about compensation and W9/invoice; send immediately after call (can take up to 3 weeks for Newry to send check)

## POST-INTERVIEW

### Thank-You and Follow-Up
- Send thank-you email within ~24 hours; include payment details if applicable
- Connect with interviewees on LinkedIn afterward
- If paying directly (not via AlphaSights or Bridger): include blank W9 (or W-8 for non-US) and invoice template

### Interview Tracking
- Track in Airtable or Excel; Newry template available (ask Adam)
- Track at minimum: project details (name, code), SME details (name, contact info, title, org, LinkedIn), interview details (date, paid/amount, client confidentiality, Newry contact), keywords describing expert's areas of expertise
- Mark Super SMEs: used repeatedly or recently for industry expertise or specific capabilities

### Interview Notes — Standards

**Bad notes (avoid):**
- No header (who, what project, what date)
- No context (what were you trying to understand?)
- No details (vague summaries without numbers, names, specifics)
- Cannot follow without significant effort

**OK notes:**
- Full header with project code, date, SME name/title/contact, Newry team members
- Structured content but could be more detailed

**Client-ready notes (target):**
- Get as close to a transcript as possible
- Full SME information
- High-level summary and key points at top
- Newry contacts listed
- Target info clearly identified
- Detailed, organized notes that capture key details including numbers, names, market sizing, company names
- One interview per Word document; saved in Primary Research folder in SharePoint

### Synthesize Immediately
- Hold ~15 minutes after each interview to:
  - Tidy and clean notes so they are client-ready
  - Write interview summaries and list key points
  - Note quick follow-up questions for thank-you note
  - Update Airtable and sync notes to SharePoint
- Findings can inform changes to interview guides
- Much easier to tidy notes one at a time than all at once later

## SYNTHESIS / CODING

### When to Code
Decision flowchart:
1. Does project rely chiefly on primary research workstreams (e.g., VOC interviews)? → YES → continue
2. Will you gather multiple interviews from each segment of the value chain? → NO → selective coding (standardize a few questions per segment, code only those)
3. Is this a SONAR briefing or "quick hit" with only 1–3 interviews? → YES → don't code (n is too small)
4. Default if yes to 1 and 2: standardize the bulk of interview guide and code across all interviews

Be strategic — define key questions to answer quantitatively *before* starting primary research; you may not need to code all interviews or all portions.

### Interview Coding Activities
- **Characterizing and tagging data:** Look across sources; tag responses with a code; a response may fit into more than one code
- **Creating a codebook:** Guide for all codes; can include demographic data; structure depends on project requirements; iterative process
- **Identifying themes:** Compare codes across dataset; identify recurring patterns and differences across subgroups; thematic analysis enables rich, quantitative output

### Tools for Coding
- **Word:** Comments or selective highlighting for initial codes in interview notes
- **Excel / Airtable:** Codebook and analysis; Airtable offers more flexibility for applying multiple codes to a single data point
- **Visualization:** Various options — refer to Kasey's Feb. 21, 2020 Knowledge Nugget for ideas

For more on interview coding: SharePoint > Consulting Resources > Knowledge Nuggets > Nuggets > Qualitative Research Series

### Minor Adjustments if Coding
- Conduct more interviews to increase sample size; strive for breadth and balance across stakeholders/demographic groups/companies
- Standardize a portion of interview guide; identify key questions to ask of all interviewees while allowing flexibility
- Spend time as team synthesizing/coding and reviewing codebook; iterative process, more time upfront but saves time during slide creation

## "MONEY SLIDE" CONCEPT
- Identify early (during project launch or right after kickoff) what chart you're driving toward — the one slide or graph client needs to answer their question
- Use to focus on most important parts of issue tree; helps ensure interview guide is crafted accordingly
- Don't be too biased — hypothesize framing but listen for unexpected themes; revisit money slide and interview guides earnestly after first few interviews and adjust accordingly
