# SoF Skill Test Cases

Compiled from Newry SharePoint project files for testing the SoF Toolkit skill.
Four decks reviewed across three project types. Key observation: only two of four decks
use the traditional Pyramid Principle SoF format — the others use OKR or module-overview
structures. Notes on each are included below.

---

## Test Case 1: Glass Core Cost Analysis (Reference Example)
**Project:** COR738-adjacent (Glass Core)  
**Project Type:** Technical/Market Landscape → Opportunity Assessment  
**Update Type:** Final executive/client readout (December 2025)  
**Deck:** 2025.12.15 Newry Glass Core Cost Analysis – Update – Final Revised.pptx  
**SoF Slide:** Slide 3, labeled "SUMMARY OF FINDINGS"

**Headline:**  
> Glass cores could save $410–580 per unit from interposer elimination or $100+ per unit from yield gains; initial CapEx and mfrg. ramps will delay benefit

**Bullets:**
- Glass is required for advanced performance: Organic cores are nearing physical and thermal limits (>800W, >3GHz, >80–100mm/side packages); glass enables finer line/space and has lower warpage and higher planarity, which are key to sustaining yield
- The broad ecosystem is moving toward glass but not yet fully aligned: Substrate makers are investing in glass core R&D; OSATs are exploring advanced handling and automation; adoption pacing hinges on ecosystem readiness and availability of data proving glass's economic value
- Organic build-up substrates are technically complex but a small share of cost: Hopper's organic build-up substrates are ~$165–220 (on average, 4% of total device cost); substrate cost is driven by copper metallization and high-density ABF build-up layers, and the organic core laminate is ~$16–33
- Yield and interposer simplification are driving most of glass's value: From modeling Hopper base case, eliminating the silicon interposer could save a total of $410–580 per unit for the OSAT; an alternate, but less likely, scenario keeps the interposer but offers warpage-related yield gains that could translate to >$100 per unit
- Further work is needed to quantify switching costs: Substrate manufacturers will spend more on glass core fabs (even up to a quarter to a third more CapEx), and yield reductions in early manufacturing are anticipated; impact of other process changes is not yet quantified; a preliminary estimate suggests glass core substrate may be able to justify premiums of ~80% or more

**Notes:** Designated as the reference "strong SoF" example by Sylvan. Headline is quantified, complete, and captures the key tension (savings potential vs. delay). Five bullets each lead with a bolded claim. Strong candidate for testing evaluate mode — should score well against the standard.

## Test Case 2: Data Center Cooling (COR727)
**Project:** COR727 Data Center Cooling 2024 CTO  
**Project Type:** Technical/Market Landscape → Opportunity Assessment  
**Update Type:** Final executive/client readout (November 2024)  
**Deck:** Data Center Cooling Update 11072024 Final.pptx  
**SoF Slide:** Slide 5, labeled "SUMMARY OF FINDINGS"

**Headline:**  
> Liquid cooling for data centers is rapidly growing, but Corning's capabilities do not fit near-term opportunities; capability development is required to participate

**Bullets:**
- The heat loads generated by the adoption of new computer chip designs, largely driven by the need to support growing AI workloads, is quickly exceeding the limits of incumbent air-cooling technologies to manage; liquid-cooling technologies are being rapidly adopted to meet this need and will represent a multi-billion-dollar opportunity by 2030
- After brainstorming potential opportunities and narrowing down based on industry SME and Corning feedback, Newry grouped them into three categories based on recommended next steps:
  - *Draft and Test Solution Concepts:* Cold Plates, Microfluidics, 3D Chip Cooling
  - *Assess Ceramics Portfolio Fit:* Heat Spreaders, TIMs
  - *No Further Work Needed:* Immersion

**Notes:** Only two bullets. The second bullet is a categorization/framework, not a finding with a claim and supporting evidence — it describes what Newry did (grouping) rather than asserting what was learned. The headline is strong (specific, captures the key tension), but the bullet structure raises questions worth exploring in evaluate mode. Good candidate for a "what's working / what to improve" test. Evaluate mode should flag the second bullet's structure as a weakness.

## Test Case 3: Power Electronics ARC (COR738)
**Project:** COR738 Power Electronics Substrates 2025  
**Project Type:** Growth Engine / Market Development  
**Update Type:** Q1 Final Update (April 2025)  
**Deck:** Power Electronics Q1 Final 04152025.pptx  
**SoF Slide:** Slides 5–6, labeled "PROGRESS & FINDINGS" (not a traditional SoF)

**Format:** OKR-style table with three objective rows:

| Objective | Findings |
|---|---|
| Identify addressable market segments in microelectronics/RF | Two automotive applications (brake pads, ultrasonic sensors) appear to be a good fit due to market size, durable performance needs, and demonstrated interest; brake pads ~400M cm² today, could exceed 1B cm² by 2030 with EV adoption; ultrasonic sensors ~2B cm² opportunity; Newry introduced Corning to TMD Friction, Brembo, Denso SME, Bosch |
| Build business case spreadsheet for power electronics | Drafts of business case and marketing materials delivered; refinements and scenario development ongoing |
| Create marketing messages 1-pager | Drafts delivered; refinements ongoing |

**Notes:** This deck does not contain a traditional Pyramid Principle SoF. The "PROGRESS & FINDINGS" format is organized around project objectives rather than synthesized findings, and there is no synthesizing headline. This is a Growth Engine / ongoing engagement project where OKR-style progress reporting may be the convention. **The SoF toolkit may not apply directly here** — or, more usefully, this is a test case for whether the skill recognizes the format difference and responds helpfully (e.g., offers to help draft a traditional SoF if one is needed, or evaluates what's there with appropriate framing).

## Test Case 4: Thin Triple-Pane Windows (COR740)
**Project:** COR740 Thin Triple 2025 EMT  
**Project Type:** Opportunity Assessment / Market Research  
**Update Type:** Final (March 27, 2025)  
**Deck:** Corning ATG Q1 2025 Final Amended.pptx  
**SoF Slide:** Slide 10, labeled "ASSESSING THE POTENTIAL FOR THIN TRIPLE-PANE WINDOWS" (OKR format)

**Format:** OKR-style table with three objective rows:

| Objective | Findings |
|---|---|
| Refine and test value proposition with at least 3 of the top 5 builders | 3 of top 5 builders interviewed; all interested in thin triple; reasons vary (Energy Star cost savings, IECC compliance, weight advantage for pre-fab); other builders interested in anticipation of future requirements; some "walked away" from ES v3.2 as too costly |
| Determine requirements to serve US homebuilders | Req 1: Lower total project cost ("most bang for the buck" per climate zone); Req 2: Proximity of window distributor to build sites; Req 3: Known suppliers with sufficient capacity |
| Estimate volume adoption / 10-year revenue forecast | Forecast adoption is $93M by 2035 from builders wanting to cost-effectively meet ERI targets (Energy Star or IECC); equivalent to 5.2M windows or ~235k homes; builders indicated ~6-month ramp-up to validate performance before rollout |

**Notes:** Like COR738, this is an OKR-style progress format rather than a traditional SoF. The project did produce strong quantified findings ($93M forecast, clear builder requirements) that could be synthesized into a strong traditional SoF — but the current slide doesn't do that synthesis. There is no synthesizing headline. The findings are rich, but they're presented objective-by-objective rather than ordered by importance. **Good candidate for testing Draft mode**: given the raw findings here, the skill should be able to draft a strong traditional SoF.

## Test Case 5: Crest Growth Strategy Module 2 (STLX01)
**Project:** STLX01 Crest Product & Market Growth Strategy  
**Project Type:** Growth Engine / Growth Strategy  
**Update Type:** Final Module 2 Update (March 17, 2026)  
**Deck:** 2026.03.17 Crest Module 2 Final Update.pptx  
**SoF Slide:** Slide 4, labeled "MODULE 2 OVERVIEW"

**Headline (slide title):**  
> Over ~14 weeks, Newry generated $4.9M in potential revenue for Crest, uncovered market insights, and delivered an action plan for additional growth

**Structured bullets by category:**

*Impact & ROI:*
- Generated 70 leads, resulting in $4.9M in potential revenue across business units
- Created ~$8M of incremental enterprise value at an 8x multiple
- Increased website traffic by 290% on average; peaking at 685% in November
- Reached >12k unique contacts >30k times, increasing mindshare
- Built foundational marketing and sales assets (one-pager, sales decision trees)

*Insights:*
- Crest has a mindshare gap (90% of leads never heard of the brand); interest emerges with education, but it is difficult to reach the key decision maker
- Focus on industrial, aerospace & defense, and packaging where lead conversion per company contacted is highest (>8%); medical and consumer had low yields (<2%) despite high outreach
- Despite long sales cycles, ~30% of leads plan to buy within 12 months; near-term revenue entry points from recurring consumables demand exists

*Recommendations & Next Steps:*
- Newry identified actionable levers to increase brand awareness and drive adoption and reviewed them with the sales team
- There are >8k untapped contacts for Newry to engage with, utilizing a trained 3rd-party calling house

**Notes:** This uses a categorized module-overview format common in Growth Engine engagements. The slide headline is actually well-quantified and assertive — it functions like a pyramid headline. The structure differs from the traditional SoF (three labeled categories vs. a flat set of bullets), but the intellectual work is there: claims are specific, there are numbers throughout, and the content is organized by type of finding. **Interesting evaluate-mode test**: does the skill recognize what's strong (specific quantified headline, claim-driven bullets) while flagging what's structurally different (category labels may cause the bullets under each to be support-oriented rather than claim-oriented)?

## Cross-Cutting Observations

1. **Only 2 of 5 decks use a traditional Pyramid Principle SoF** (Glass Core and Data Center Cooling). The other three use OKR, module-overview, or progress-table formats.

2. **Project type correlates with format**: Opportunity Assessment / Technical Landscape projects tend to use traditional SoF. Growth Engine / Market Development projects tend to use OKR or module formats.

3. **Implication for the skill**: The SoF Toolkit should be robust to different input formats. When a consultant pastes in a progress table or module overview, the skill should either evaluate it against what a traditional SoF would look like for that context, or offer to draft one if the format isn't there yet.

4. **Richest test cases for evaluate mode**: Glass Core (good), Data Center Cooling (mixed — good headline, weak second bullet), Crest Module 2 (structurally different but intellectually strong).

5. **Richest test cases for draft mode**: Thin Triple (strong underlying findings but no SoF), Power Electronics ARC (findings emerging but project still in-progress at time of update).
