## Test Case: Data Center Cooling — SoF Run (old skill)

**Source:** Data Center Cooling Interim Update 09112024 Final Jaymin.pptx
**Objectives slide:** Not provided
**SoF evaluated:** Traditional SoF (headline + 2 bullets)
**Stage / Audience:** Final executive/client readout
**Input type:** Pasted text

---

### Quick Take

- **Headline:** Strong — captures core tension (growing market, Corning gap) and lands a conclusion; honest, doesn't oversell
- **Bullet 1:** Market context posing as a finding — liquid cooling growth belongs in the intro, not the SoF
- **Bullet 2:** Methodology sentence — describes what Newry did, not what Newry found; the finding is buried in the sub-bullets
- **Structural:** Two bullets is thin for a final readout; six technologies evaluated in the body, almost none synthesized in the SoF
- **Overall:** Headline earns its position; bullets don't

---

### Full Eval

**What's working**

- Headline makes a real assertion — captures the core tension (large and growing market, structural Corning gap) and lands a clear conclusion (capability development required). Honest: doesn't oversell a near-term opportunity that doesn't exist.

**Top 3 improvements**

- *Bullet 2 is a methodology sentence, not a finding.* "Newry grouped them into three categories based on recommended next steps" tells the executive that Newry organized things into buckets — not what the insight behind the groupings is. A stronger version leads with what Corning's fit actually looks like: "Cold plates and microfluidics offer the clearest paths for concept development, but heat spreaders and TIMs would require expanding Corning's ceramics portfolio, and immersion offers no viable entry point for solid materials."
- *Bullet 1 is market context, not a Corning-specific finding.* Liquid cooling growing to multi-billion by 2030 is background that motivated the project — it doesn't tell Corning what they learned about their specific situation. SoF bullets should be Corning-specific conclusions. A sharper version quantifies the opportunity specifically for Corning, names the capability gaps, or characterizes what "capability development" requires.
- *Two bullets is structurally thin for a final readout.* The body has six technologies evaluated, quantified market sizes, specific competitive players, Corning fit assessments — almost none of that synthesis appears in the SoF. A reader with only the SoF would know "Corning doesn't fit near-term" but not which opportunities are worth pursuing, what the prize is if capabilities are developed, or what specific gaps need addressing.

**Headline rewrite**

> "The data center liquid cooling market will exceed $5B by 2030, but Corning's glass and ceramic materials cannot compete near-term; cold plates and microfluidics are the strongest longer-term concepts to develop, pending feasibility work"

**Consistency pass**

- Headline doesn't introduce figures not in the deck. ✓
- Only two bullets for a six-technology analysis — gap between SoF depth and body depth flagged but not resolved.

---

### Observations on Skill Performance

- Correctly distinguished a methodology sentence from a finding (Bullet 2)
- Recognized background/context posing as a finding (Bullet 1)
- Caught structural thinness — SoF doesn't reflect the depth of the body analysis

## Test Case: Crest Module 2 — SoF Run (old skill)

**Source:** Growth Engine engagement (STLX01)
**Objectives slide:** Not provided
**SoF evaluated:** Module overview format — three labeled categories (Impact & ROI / Insights / Recommendations & Next Steps)
**Stage / Audience:** Final executive/client readout
**Input type:** Pasted text

---

### Quick Take

- **Format:** Module overview (three labeled categories); appropriate for Growth Engine but trades away importance ordering
- **Headline:** Strong — specific, quantified, right instinct to lead with revenue for an execution engagement
- **Structure:** Three-category format buries the most important finding (segment prioritization) behind campaign statistics
- **Recommendations:** Too thin — "actionable levers identified" without naming the most important one
- **Activity vs. findings:** Some bullets describe what Newry did, not what Crest should conclude
- **Overall:** Rich underlying content; structural fix needed more than a content fix

---

### Full Eval

**What's working**

- Headline is specific, quantified, and right for the project type — for a Growth Engine engagement where the deliverable is execution results, leading with $4.9M revenue is the correct call. The 8x enterprise value amplifier works.
- Underlying content is rich: segment conversion rates (>8% for industrial, <2% for medical), 90% mindshare gap, 30% near-term buy intent — exactly the kind of numbers that belong in a SoF.

**Top 3 improvements**

- *Three-category structure buries the most important finding.* The segmentation conclusion — industrial, A&D, and packaging are the right segments; medical/consumer are not — is buried mid-slide after campaign statistics. Category structure forces sequential reading instead of importance-first ordering. Some "Insights" bullets are more important than some "Impact & ROI" bullets; the buckets override the logic.
- *Recommendations bucket is too thin.* "Newry identified actionable levers" and ">8k untapped contacts" tells the client next steps exist, not what the most important one is. A final readout should leave no ambiguity: which lever matters most, which segment first, what happens in the next 90 days.
- *Some bullets describe activity, not findings.* ">12k unique contacts reached >30k times" and "foundational marketing and sales assets built" recount what Newry did. The finding is what that activity produced — a validated conversion rate and a segment prioritization.

**On the format**

The three-category structure isn't wrong for a Growth Engine module overview. If staying in it: resequence within each bucket by importance and sharpen Recommendations. If moving to a traditional SoF: flatten to 5–6 bullets, segment prioritization near the top.

**Headline rewrite**

> "Newry generated $4.9M in pipeline and validated that industrial, A&D, and packaging are Crest's highest-conversion segments; >8k untapped contacts remain and a 3rd-party calling house can scale what's working"

**Consistency pass**

- $4.9M and >8k untapped contacts appear in both headline and body. ✓
- Segmentation claim (industrial/A&D/packaging) supported by specific conversion rates in Insights. ✓

---

### Observations on Skill Performance

- Handled non-traditional format without forcing it into the wrong mold — acknowledged format and evaluated what was there
- Correctly distinguished activity bullets from finding bullets
- Format acceptance question: Crest module overview is arguably legitimate for a Growth Engine update — the eval was useful but could acknowledge this more directly

## Test Case: Glass Core Value Prop 12.15.25 deck — SoF Run 4.13.26

**Source:** SoF Examples.pptx (not the amr version)
**Objectives slide:** Slide 1 — Glass Core Cost Analysis background and objectives
**SoF evaluated:** Slide 2 — traditional SoF (headline + bullets)
**Stage / Audience:** Final client deliverable (2025.12.15 Glass Core Cost Analysis — Final Revised)
**Input type:** Text extracted from PPTX

---

### Quick Take

- **Headline:** Strong — quantified dual-scenario claim, honest hedging, fits character target; minor: "mfrg." is non-standard
- **Bullets 4–5:** Best-structured bullets in any eval so far — claims lead, numbers support, Bullet 5 honest about open questions
- **Bullets 1–3:** Context leads, claim buried — particularly Bullet 3, which is close to a real finding but doesn't state it
- **Objective 1 gap:** "Quantify the performance threshold for transition" was the first objective; the SoF doesn't answer it
- **Bullet 5 ambiguity:** 80% premium has no reference point — could mean $13–27 or $130–180 depending on what the base is
- **Overall:** Strongest SoF evaluated; weaknesses are narrow

---

### Full Eval

**What's working**

- Headline leads with a quantified claim ($410–580 vs. $100+), uses "or" accurately for mutually exclusive scenarios, and lands the switching cost caveat in the second clause — all within the character target. The "could" hedging is appropriate given the scenario-based analysis.
- Bullet 4 is the best-structured bullet in any eval so far: bolded claim leads, both scenarios quantified, likelihood distinguished ("alternate, but less likely"). This is what a finding looks like.
- Bullet 5 is clean — honest about incompleteness, quantified where possible (quarter to a third more CapEx), ends with a pricing implication.
- Objectives 2a–2c (substrate cost, yield economics, CapEx) are all covered.

**Top 3 improvements**

- *Bullets 1–3 lead with context, not claims.* Bullet 1 describes thermal limits Corning already understands; Bullet 2 is market status; Bullet 3 sets up the value analysis without stating it. All three do real work as setup, but the claim should lead. Bullet 3 is closest to a finding — "the substrate laminate is a small share of device cost (~$16–33 of $165–220), meaning glass's value rests on yield and interposer impacts, not substrate displacement" — the current framing buries that conclusion.
- *Objective 1 unanswered.* The background slide asks to "quantify the performance threshold that will force a transition from organic to glass core." The SoF mentions >800W, >3GHz, >80–100mm/side as the limits where organic cores fail, but never synthesizes a threshold: when does a customer cross it? Which device categories cross it first? This was Objective 1.
- *80% premium (Bullet 5) has no reference point.* 80% above the core laminate (~$16–33) = $13–27 premium. 80% above the full substrate (~$165–220) = $130–180. These are an order of magnitude apart, and Corning may use this figure in pricing discussions.

**Headline rewrite**

Headline doesn't need structural change. One clean-up only — "mfrg." → "manufacturing":
> "Glass cores could save $410–580 per unit from interposer elimination or $100+ from yield gains; initial CapEx and manufacturing ramp will delay benefit"

(~152 characters)

**Consistency pass**

- $410–580 and $100+ in headline match Bullet 4 exactly. ✓
- Bullet 3 substrate cost ($165–220, 4% of device cost) implies total device cost ~$4,125–5,500 for Hopper — plausible. ✓
- "Or" in headline accurately reflects that interposer elimination and yield-gain scenarios are mutually exclusive in Bullet 4. ✓
- Objective 1 (transition threshold) named in objectives slide — absent from SoF.
- 80% premium in Bullet 5 — reference base unstated.

---

### Observations on Skill Performance

- Correctly validated a strong SoF without generating false positives — the "what's working" section is substantive, not faint praise
- Catching the Objective 1 gap required grounding in the objectives slide; without slide 1, the SoF would appear more complete than it is. Further evidence that providing the objectives slide materially sharpens the eval.
- The 80% premium ambiguity is a precision issue the skill caught that a quick read-through would likely miss

## Test Case: Windows Mkt Sizing Segmentation 4.2.26 deck — SoF Run 4.13.26

**Source:** SoF Examples.pptx (not the amr version)
**Objectives slide:** Slide 15 — Advanced Windows Q1 OKRs (OKR progress table)
**SoF evaluated:** Slide 16 — "Summary of Findings by Market"
**Key result under evaluation:** "Size market by revenue and segment by sales channels and end customer in the US and Canada" (North America / Optimize Glass Pricing; status: In progress)
**Stage / Audience:** Final update, client-facing (04.02.2026 Q1 OKR Update)
**Input type:** Text extracted from PPTX

---

### Quick Take

- **Format:** "Summary of Findings by Market" — geographic with TAM/SAM/SOM sub-structure; appropriate for a market sizing key result
- **Headline:** False equivalence — Canada 1.2M ft2 = SOM (via AWG); US ~12M ft2 = SAM; body labels them correctly, headline collapses them into parallel claims
- **TAM bullets:** Context, not claims — neither US nor Canada TAM bullet states a Corning-specific conclusion
- **Canada SOM growth:** 61k ft2 in 2026 → 1.2M ft2 in 2030 (~20x) — growth driver not explained anywhere on the slide
- **Objective gap:** Key result says "by revenue"; slide uses ft2 throughout with no revenue figure
- **Overall:** Strong structure for market sizing; headline and TAM bullets need the most work

---

### Full Eval

**What's working**

- TAM/SAM/SOM layering is logical for a market sizing key result — gives Corning a layered view that matches how they think about prioritization.
- Canada SOM bullet is the strongest element: quantified (61k ft2 in 2026 → 1.2M ft2 in 2030), specific to the AWG relationship, directly answers what Corning cares about most.
- Provincial breakdown (Prairie provinces ~80% triple-pane vs. Quebec ~40%) is a genuine finding — tells Corning and AWG where to focus, not just how big the market is.

**Top 3 improvements**

- *Headline conflates different funnel levels.* Canada 1.2M ft2 by 2030 is a SOM (Corning's share via AWG). US ~12M ft2 annually is a SAM (Corning's total Enlighten opportunity across all partners). The body labels these correctly; the headline presents them as parallel. A reader would conclude the US is ~10x Canada — but that's SAM vs. SOM, not a like-for-like comparison.
- *TAM bullets are context, not claims.* "~56M windows projected in 2026; expected to decline -0.2%" and "~5.4M windows in Canada; Alberta/Ontario/Quebec are largest markets" describe the market without stating a Corning-specific conclusion. The US TAM bullet is building toward something — that low, stagnant triple-pane penetration makes Corning's US opportunity nearly flat regardless of market size — but never says it.
- *Canada SOM growth driver is absent.* 61k ft2 in 2026 → 1.2M ft2 in 2030 is roughly 20x growth. No driver is explained — AWG's assumed market share trajectory, geographic expansion, adoption changes. This is the number a client will push on hardest, and the slide can't support the pushback.

**Headline rewrite**

> "Canada's AWG partnership defines a clear SOM of 1.2M ft2 by 2030; the US SAM is larger (~12M ft2) but remains untapped without a comparable go-to-market partner"

(~161 characters — fits the 150–175 target; original at ~110 characters left substance on the table; both funnel levels named explicitly)

**Consistency pass**

- Canada 1.2M ft2 and US 12M ft2 in headline match the body. ✓
- Body labels them correctly as SOM and SAM; headline presents them as parallel — internal inconsistency.
- Canada SOM: 20x growth in four years with no driver stated — single biggest consistency risk on the slide.
- Key result specifies "by revenue"; slide uses ft2 throughout — gap between stated objective and delivered metric.

---

### Observations on Skill Performance

- Funnel-level mismatch in the headline (SOM vs. SAM as equivalents) is a new failure mode — subtle because the body labels them correctly. The skill caught it.
- The headline rewrite initially reproduced the problem in subtler form (labeled US as "SAM," left Canada unlabeled). Corrected in final version.
- "By revenue" gap was only catchable because the objectives slide was provided — further evidence that grounding the eval in stated objectives materially sharpens the output.
- TAM/SAM/SOM geographic structure accepted as appropriate for a market sizing answer without penalizing for non-traditional format. Consistent with prior format handling.

## Test Case: Lucas Oil Phase 2 Interim Update — Align Run 4.13.26

**Source:** 2024.05.16 Lucas Oil - Phase 2 Interim Update - DRAFT.pptx
**SoF slide:** Slide 5 — "Preliminary Summary of Findings"
**Body slides reviewed:** Slides 6–15
**Stage / Audience:** Phase 2 Interim update, preliminary, client-facing

---

### Quick Take

- **Format:** Preliminary interim update; lower coverage bar applies — findings so far, not full synthesis
- **Headline:** Accurate directionally but understates the prerequisite — forging partnerships requires meeting distributor portfolio requirements first
- **Bullet 1:** Factory Fill presented as active sub-segment; body has largely deprioritized it (HE factory fill deprioritized; auto factory fill still exploring)
- **Missing:** CaS gaining share at 25% (Slide 7) — relevant to Lucas's product planning, not in SoF
- **Missing:** Specific distributor portfolio requirements (Slide 14) — multiple thickeners, bases, viscosities, packaging types needed to be carried at all
- **Overall:** Core recommendation holds; two gaps worth flagging — Factory Fill framing and portfolio breadth requirements

---

### Full Eval

**Matches well**

- Li grease dominance at 60–70% of volume, sold in drums, through distributor networks ✓ (Slides 7, 8, 10 — "99% of MRO products go through industrial distributors")
- Market fragmentation, performance as primary selection criterion ✓ (Slides 10, 13 — Grease Formulation HIGH, Availability MEDIUM, Price LOW)
- Distributor strategy — inventory, lead times, technical support, rebates over discounts ✓ (Slide 15 directly supports all four, including rebates of 5–35%)

**Missing from SoF**

- *CaS gaining share at 25% (Slide 7).* Li complex dominates but CaS is growing, driven by performance and price. Relevant to Lucas's product portfolio decisions — "broad inventory" in Bullet 4 implicitly requires carrying CaS, but the trend isn't stated.
- *Specific distributor portfolio requirements (Slide 14).* Distributors expect multiple thickeners (Li, CaS, polyurea), bases, viscosities, NLGI grades, and packaging types. The SoF says "broad inventory" without defining what breadth means — and the body makes clear Lucas must meet these requirements to be carried at all, before any partnership strategy applies.
- *Supplier ratings beyond rebates (Slide 15).* Distributors rate suppliers on warranty, on-time delivery, and pricing vs. benchmarks in addition to rebates and support. The SoF captures rebates and technical support but omits warranty and delivery reliability.

**Overstated / misstated**

- *Bullet 1 presents Factory Fill as an active sub-segment alongside MRO.* The body (Slide 6) shows automotive Factory Fill as "still exploring" and heavy equipment Factory Fill as "deprioritized during scoping." A reader of the SoF would think Factory Fill is a live consideration.

**Consistency pass**

- Li grease at 60–70% ✓ matches Slide 7
- All four distributor strategy elements ✓ appear in Slide 15
- Factory Fill framing inconsistency: SoF presents it as active; body has largely set it aside
- Stage note: Phase 2 Interim/preliminary — lower coverage bar applies

---

### Observations on Skill Performance

- Align mode works well for an interim deck — the "matches well" section is substantive, and the gaps are specific rather than generic
- Factory Fill framing gap is a good example of a subtle inconsistency: not wrong in the body, just not reflected accurately in the SoF
- Portfolio breadth requirement gap shows the skill can catch when a SoF recommendation ("broad inventory") is underspecified relative to what the body actually defines

---

### Quick Take

The SoF reports the gross yield benefit without accounting for switching costs. The body explicitly models Scenario 2 as potentially uneconomic. This needs correction.
- *Bullet 5's "~80% or more" premium.* Slide 16 shows Scenario 1 WTP at $295–575 over an organic substrate price of ~$165–220, implying 79–161% premium. The 80% figure aligns with the low end of Scenario 1 but is presented as a standalone preliminary estimate rather than a scenario-specific range.

**Consistency pass**

- $410–580 and $100+ figures match Slides 9 and 16 ✓ (but Scenario 2 note applies)
- CapEx multiplier matches "quarter to a third more" ✓
- **Scenario 2 WTP in SoF (>$100 gross savings) conflicts with Slide 17 (net WTP can be negative)**
- 80% premium sits at low end of body's modeled range; spread not acknowledged

---

### Observations on Skill Performance

- Align mode caught a material accuracy issue (Scenario 2 WTP) that Evaluate mode missed — the Evaluate run flagged the 80% premium ambiguity but didn't catch the Scenario 2 net-negative WTP because the body slides weren't available
- This is the clearest case so far where having the full deck changes the evaluation outcome: the SoF's Scenario 2 characterization looks reasonable in isolation but is contradicted by Slide 17
- The tipping point gap reinforces the Objective 1 finding from the Evaluate run — the answer exists in the deck (Slide 4) but doesn't surface in the SoF
- Align mode and Evaluate mode are genuinely complementary: Evaluate catches what the SoF doesn't say; Align catches where what it says diverges from what the body shows
