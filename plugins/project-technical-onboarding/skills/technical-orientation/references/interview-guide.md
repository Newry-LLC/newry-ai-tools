# Interview Guide

Detailed question bank, follow-up probes, and guidance on what good looks like for each section of the Technical Orientation interview.

---

## Section 1: Product & Technology

**Opening question:**
"Walk me through what [client]'s product actually does — not the marketing version, but how it functionally works. What is it, what does it do, and how does it do it?"

**Follow-up probes:**
- "What's the core underlying technology or process? How does it actually produce the outcome?"
- "What are the key performance parameters — the specs or metrics that matter most to customers? Purity grades, yield, output capacity, efficiency, tolerance levels — whatever's relevant here."
- "What are the product's known limitations or failure modes? What doesn't it do well?"
- "How is this product made or delivered — what does the production or service process look like, and does that matter for understanding where [client] has an advantage?"

**What good looks like:**
The consultant can explain the product clearly to someone unfamiliar with the sector. The explanation covers function, mechanism, and key performance dimensions — not just product category. It acknowledges limitations, not just strengths.

**Common gaps to push on:**
- Vague descriptions that apply to any product in the category ("it provides advanced solutions for...")
- Inability to describe the technical mechanism, only the outcome
- No awareness of performance specifications or relevant metrics
- No acknowledgment of what the product doesn't do well

**Collaboration guidance:**
If you have knowledge of the technology category, offer a framing hypothesis: "My understanding is that this type of product typically works by X — is that right for [client]'s approach, and where do they differ?" This accelerates the conversation and tests the consultant's depth.

## Section 2: Value Creation

This is the analytical core. Work through the four sub-sections in order. The goal is to understand where [client] sits in the value chain, what their technology enables downstream, whether their value creation is differentiated or commoditized, and who else creates similar value.

### 2.1 Value Chain Position

**Questions:**
- "Where does [client] sit in the value chain — raw material supplier, component manufacturer, subsystem integrator, OEM, service provider, something else?"
- "Who are their direct customers? And who are their customers' customers?"
- "What does [client]'s product enable downstream — what becomes possible at the system level because of it?"

**What good looks like:**
A clear picture of where [client] sits, who they sell to, and what their product enables two levels downstream. Not just "they sell to industrial companies" but "they sell to X, who uses it to make Y, which enables Z performance in the end product."

**Common gaps to push on:**
- Knowing only the direct customer without understanding the downstream chain
- No sense of what the product enables at the system level (the "so what")

---

### 2.2 How Value Is Created

Map [client]'s technology against the four value creation categories. For each one, the question is whether [client]'s product creates value through this mechanism, and if so, how specifically.

**System Performance Gains**
- "Does [client]'s technology improve capability, output, speed, precision, or functionality relative to alternatives? What specifically gets better, and by how much?"

**System Efficiency Gains**
- "Does it reduce resource use, waste, weight, energy consumption, or labor? Where is the efficiency created — in the customer's process, in the end product's operation, both?"

**System Reliability / Risk Reduction**
- "Does it reduce failure rates, defects, downtime, or compliance risk? Is this the primary value driver, or secondary?"

**Market Expansion / Demand Creation**
- "Does this technology unlock new applications, segments, or customer categories that couldn't be served before? Is [client] enabling something that wasn't previously possible?"

**Synthesis prompt (after mapping all four):**
"Of these four, which is the primary mechanism of value creation for [client]? Which are secondary? If you had to write one sentence about what value [client] creates and for whom, what would it be?"

**What good looks like:**
The consultant can identify the primary value creation mechanism and be specific about it — not just "it improves performance" but "it reduces X by Y%, which matters because downstream customers need Z." Multiple mechanisms are fine; the goal is to know which one is primary and why.

**Common gaps to push on:**
- Claiming all four categories without being able to support any of them with specifics
- Stating the value mechanism without any sense of magnitude
- Confusing what [client] markets with what customers actually care about

---

### 2.3 Differentiated vs. Commodity

**Framing question (ask first):**
"Is this primarily a differentiated-technology play — where [client] wins because their product does something competitors can't match — or more of a commodity-plus-business play, where the technology is table stakes and they win on the business side?"

This is a diagnostic question. Help the consultant think it through rather than accepting a quick answer. Many clients operate in hybrid territory; push for specificity about where differentiation exists and where it doesn't.

**For differentiated-tech plays:**
- "Where exactly does [client] win technically? What can they do that competitors can't — and how durable is that advantage?"
- "What is the source of the technical differentiation — materials, process, IP, proprietary know-how, scale, regulatory position?"
- "How defensible is it? Could a well-funded competitor replicate it in 2–3 years?"
- "Where are they technically weaker relative to alternatives?"

*What good looks like:* Specific claims about technical advantage grounded in performance specs or customer outcomes, an honest view of durability, and acknowledgment of where competitors are close or ahead.

**For commodity-plus-business plays:**

Build the table stakes checklist collaboratively: "What are the minimum technical requirements a product must meet to even be considered by buyers in this market?"

For each spec: What's the threshold? Is [client] comfortably above it, just meeting it, or at risk?

| Spec / Capability | Market Threshold | Client Position |
|---|---|---|
| [Spec 1] | [Threshold] | Above / At / Below |
| [Spec 2] | [Threshold] | Above / At / Below |

Then: "Given they're not winning on unique technology, where do they actually win? What is the real source of their competitive position?"

Probe: pricing, delivery reliability, service model, customer relationships, distribution, speed, customization capability, brand, regulatory positioning.

*What good looks like:* An explicit acknowledgment that the differentiation is commercial/operational, a clear-eyed table stakes assessment, and a specific view of where the business advantage lies.

**Common gaps to push on:**
- Claiming differentiation without being able to name a specific performance dimension
- "We make a good product" treated as a defensible position
- Hybrid cases where the consultant hasn't pushed to separate what's differentiated from what's table stakes

### 2.4 Competitive Value Creation

**Opening question:**
"Who else in the value chain creates similar value? Both direct competitors and substitute technologies or adjacent solutions."

**Follow-up probes:**
- "For each main competitor: what is their technical approach — same technology, different technology, or a different solution to the same problem?"
- "At which value chain positions do competitors operate? Are there players upstream or downstream from [client] that create competing value?"
- "Where is [client] genuinely ahead, at parity, and behind on value creation?"
- "Are there emerging players or substitute technologies that could change the picture in 3–5 years?"

**Format prompt:**
Build a simple comparison view: rows are competitors (including [client]), columns are key technical dimensions from Section 1. Fill in as you discuss.

**What good looks like:**
A grounded, specific view of the competitive set — not a list of company names, but an actual technical comparison with a clear read on where [client] leads, where they're at parity, and where they trail.

**Common gaps to push on:**
- "They're the market leader" without specifics on what they lead on
- Dismissing competitors without evidence
- No awareness of emerging or substitute technologies
- Conflating market share with technical superiority

## Section 3: Value Capture

**Framing:**
"Now let's think about who actually captures the value we just described — how much of it flows to [client], and what drives or limits their ability to monetize."

### 3.1 Value Capture Across the Chain

**Questions:**
- "Who benefits most from the value this technology creates — [client], their customers, or further downstream? Where does the value actually land?"
- "How does [client] get compensated for the value they create — premium pricing, volume, cost savings sharing, licensing?"
- "Where does value 'leak' away from [client]? Who bears the cost and switching burden of adoption — and does that help or hurt [client]'s capture position?"

**What good looks like:**
A clear view of where value creation and value capture are aligned vs. misaligned. Ideally the consultant can say: "[Client] creates significant value but captures only X% of it because Y."

---

### 3.2 Customer Buying Dynamics

**Questions:**
- "Who in the customer organization makes the buy decision? Who influences it but doesn't decide?"
- "Is the value proposition primarily technical (product does something others can't) or commercial (price, service, reliability, relationship)?"
- "What would the customer do without this product — substitute, go without, build internally?"

**What good looks like:**
A clear distinction between the technical buyer, the economic buyer, and the influencers — and an honest read on what actually drives the decision. If the consultant doesn't know who the buyer is, that's an open question to flag.

**Common gaps to push on:**
- Assuming the technical buyer is the economic buyer
- "They buy it because it's the best product" without understanding what "best" means to the buyer
- No awareness of build/buy/substitute dynamics

---

### 3.3 Pricing Power and Stickiness

**Questions:**
- "Can [client] actually capture the value they create through pricing, or does competition commoditize it away?"
- "What switching costs exist once a customer is in — technical integration, qualification requirements, capital investment, relationships?"
- "How durable is [client]'s capture position? What would cause it to erode over the next 3–5 years?"

**What good looks like:**
An honest view of pricing power and its sources. The consultant should be able to say whether [client]'s capture position is strong and durable, strong but fragile, or weak and why.

**Common gaps to push on:**
- Claiming pricing power without being able to explain its source
- No awareness of what would erode capture position
- Conflating customer loyalty with switching cost

---

## Section 4: Key Engagement Questions

**Framing:**
"The last thing I want to nail down is the bridge between technical understanding and what we're actually trying to figure out on this project. Given the engagement objectives from the SOW, what technical questions — if we could answer them — would most change how the team thinks about the work?"

**Follow-up probes:**
- "Why does this question matter specifically for *this engagement* — not just as a general knowledge gap, but as something that affects our analytical approach or recommendations?"
- "How do you actually resolve it — client interview, secondary research, expert call, product demo?"
- "Who at [client] or in the market would you go to? Is that conversation already planned?"
- "If we can't resolve it, what are the implications? Is this a risk to flag rather than a question to answer?"

**Format:**
Capture as a table:
| Question | Why It Matters for This Engagement | How to Resolve | Owner | Target Date |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**What good looks like:**
3–6 questions that are specific, actionable, and tied directly to the engagement objectives. Each should be answerable by a named action. A consultant who can't say how to resolve a question hasn't finished the analytical thinking.

**Common gaps to push on:**
- Open questions that are vague or apply to any project in the sector
- Questions that are interesting but don't affect how the team approaches *this engagement*
- No resolution path — if a question can't be resolved, it should be reframed as a risk
- Missing questions the team will clearly depend on later (flag these proactively)

**Collaboration guidance:**
After the consultant offers their list, add your own hypothesis questions based on what you heard in Sections 2 and 3. For example: "Based on what you described about their pricing model, I'd add a question about X — here's why I think that matters for the engagement." Label these clearly as hypotheses.
