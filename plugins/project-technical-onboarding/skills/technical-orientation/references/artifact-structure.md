# Artifact Structure

The Technical Orientation document is a concise, structured .docx. Substantive but not long — the goal is a document the team will actually read and use, not an exhaustive report. Target 4–7 pages depending on engagement complexity.

---

## Document Header

- **Title:** Technical Orientation
- **Subtitle:** [Client Name] — [Project Code]
- **Prepared by:** [Consultant Name]
- **Date:** [Date]
- **Status:** Initial Draft (Week 1) — living document

---

## Summary

**Position:** Immediately after the title block, before Section 1.

**Length:** 4–6 tight bullets. No sub-headings.

**Content:** Synthesized overview of the technical landscape — not a table of contents. A colleague should read this and immediately understand: what the client's product does technically, what type of value it creates and for whom, and what the central engagement question is.

**Standard:** Write this last, after all four sections are drafted. Each bullet should carry a specific insight, not a category label. If a bullet can't be written with specificity, the underlying section needs more work.

**Format:** Bullets only. Lead with the insight, not the category.

---

## Section 1: Product & Technology

**Heading:** Product & Technology

**Purpose:** Establish a clear, technically grounded understanding of the product — what it is, how it works, and what the key performance parameters are. This is the foundation for everything that follows.

**Content:**
- What the product is and what it does at a functional level — plain language, not marketing copy
- The core technical mechanism: how does it actually produce the outcome?
- Key performance parameters: the specs and metrics that matter most — purity grades, yield, output capacity, efficiency, tolerance levels, etc. Use a table or bullets where multiple specs exist
- Known technical limitations or constraints — be honest; these shape the analytical agenda
- How the product is made or delivered, where relevant to understanding differentiation

**Tone:** Write as if explaining to an intelligent colleague who doesn't know the sector. Avoid jargon unless defined.

**Format:** Mix of prose (for mechanism and context) and bullets/tables (for specs and parameters). Use sub-headings if the product portfolio or delivery mechanism is complex enough to warrant them.

## Section 2: Value Creation

**Heading:** How [Client]'s Technology Creates Value

**Purpose:** Establish where the client sits in the value chain, how their technology creates value at the system level, whether that value creation is differentiated or commoditized, and who creates competing value. This is the analytical core of the document.

**Content and structure:**

### 2.1 Value Chain Position
- Where does the client sit in the value chain? (e.g., raw material supplier, component manufacturer, subsystem integrator, OEM, service provider)
- Who are their direct customers, and who are their customers' customers?
- What does the client's product enable downstream — what becomes possible at the system level because of it?

### 2.2 How Value Is Created
Map the client's technology against the four value creation categories:
- **System Performance Gains** — does it improve capability, output, speed, precision, or functionality?
- **System Efficiency Gains** — does it reduce resource use, waste, weight, energy, or labor?
- **System Reliability / Risk Reduction** — does it reduce failure rates, defects, downtime, or compliance risk?
- **Market Expansion / Demand Creation** — does it unlock new applications, segments, or customer categories?

For each category where value is created: be specific about the mechanism and magnitude. Cite performance dimensions, not just conclusions.

### 2.3 Differentiated vs. Commodity
Make the call: is this a **differentiated-technology play** (the client creates value others cannot replicate) or a **commodity-plus-business play** (the technology is table stakes and competitive position is commercial/operational)?

- *Differentiated:* What specifically gives the client a technical advantage? What is the source (materials, process, IP, know-how, scale, regulatory position)? How durable is it — could a well-funded competitor replicate it in 2–3 years?
- *Commodity:* Build the table stakes checklist — what are the minimum technical requirements to compete? Is the client comfortably meeting them? Where does the client actually win (service, relationships, distribution, cost, speed)?

### 2.4 Competitive Value Creation
- Who else in the value chain creates similar value? (direct competitors, substitute technologies, adjacent solutions)
- At which value chain positions do competitors operate?
- Where is the client genuinely ahead, at parity, and behind on value creation?
- Are there emerging players or substitute technologies that could change the picture in 3–5 years?

**Format:** Sub-headings as above. Use tables for table stakes checklist and competitive comparison. Use prose for analysis and interpretation.

---

## Section 3: Value Capture

**Heading:** How Value Is Captured

**Purpose:** Establish who captures the value created by the client's technology, how much of it the client retains, and what drives or limits their ability to monetize.

**Content and structure:**

### 3.1 Value Capture Across the Chain
- Who benefits most from the value this technology creates — the client, their customers, or further downstream?
- How is the client compensated for the value they create? (premium pricing, volume, cost savings, licensing, etc.)
- Who bears the cost and switching burden of adoption? Where does value "leak" away from the client?

### 3.2 Customer Buying Dynamics
- Who in the customer organization makes the buy decision vs. influences it?
- Is the value proposition primarily technical (product does something others can't) or commercial (price, service, reliability, relationship)?
- What does the customer do without this product — substitute, go without, build internally?

### 3.3 Pricing Power and Stickiness
- Can the client actually capture the value they create through pricing, or does competition commoditize it away?
- What switching costs exist once a customer is in? (technical integration, qualification requirements, capital investment, relationships)
- How durable is the client's capture position — what would cause it to erode?

**Format:** Sub-headings as above. Mix of prose and bullets. Where specific financial dynamics are known (e.g., premium pricing estimates, cost savings estimates), include them — approximate is fine.

---

## Section 4: Key Engagement Questions

**Heading:** Key Engagement Questions

**Purpose:** Close the loop between technical understanding and engagement objectives. These are not generic research gaps — they are questions whose answers materially affect how the team should approach *this engagement*, derived directly from the project SOW or proposal.

**Content:**
- Identify 3–6 questions at the intersection of technical understanding and engagement objectives
- For each: why it matters for *this specific engagement*, how it gets resolved, who resolves it, and by when
- Questions should be specific and actionable — a vague question means the underlying analysis is unfinished

**Format (table):**
| Question | Why It Matters for This Engagement | How to Resolve | Owner | Target Date |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**Standard:** Each question should be answerable by a specific action (interview, secondary research, expert call, product demo). If it can't be resolved, it's a risk to flag — not an open question to list.

---

## Formatting Notes

- US Letter, 1-inch margins
- Headings: bold, 14pt, Newry navy for H1, dark gray for H2/H3
- Body: 11pt Arial
- Tables: light gray headers, clean borders, no heavy shading
- Running header every page: "[Client] Technical Orientation — [Project Code]"
- Footer: "Draft — [Date]   Page X"
- Total length: 4–7 pages depending on engagement complexity
